What does John 3:16 mean?

3 min read

"For God so loved the world" is perhaps the most quoted verse in Scripture, yet its depth is often missed. The Greek adverb οὕτως (houtōs) does not primarily mean "so much" but rather "in this way" — pointing forward to how God loved, not merely to the intensity of that love.

How did God love the world? By giving his μονογενής (monogenēs) — his one and only, unique Son. This is not one gift among many; it is the costliest gift imaginable, the Father's own beloved. The word μονογενής stresses uniqueness: there is no other Son like this one. He is eternal, uncreated, the very Word who was God (John 1:1).

The human response is faith: πιστεύων (pisteuōn), a present participle describing ongoing, active trust — not a one-time decision but a life of reliance upon Christ. And the promise is breathtaking: not mere survival but ζωὴν αἰώνιον (zōēn aiōnion), eternal life — the very life of God shared with those who believe.

John 3:16 is, in one sentence, the entire gospel: the love of God, the gift of the Son, the call to faith, and the promise of life.

What is repentance?

3 min read

Repentance is μετάνοια (metanoia) in the Greek New Testament — literally, a change of mind that produces a change of direction. It is not mere sorrow, guilt, or emotional regret. Judas felt remorse (μεταμεληθείς, metameletheis, Matt. 27:3), but his remorse led to despair, not to God. True repentance turns toward God.

The Old Testament Hebrew word שׁוּב (shuv) enriches the picture. It means to turn, to reverse course. The prophets use it constantly: "Return to me, and I will return to you" (Mal. 3:7). Repentance is not just thinking differently; it is walking differently — turning away from sin and turning back toward the God who calls.

Repentance and faith are inseparable. They are two sides of one act of conversion. You cannot truly trust Christ while clinging to the sin he died to destroy. As Mark 1:15 records Jesus' first words of public ministry: "Repent and believe in the gospel." The order is not accidental — turning from and turning to happen together.

Martin Luther understood this. His first of the Ninety-Five Theses reads: "When our Lord and Master Jesus Christ said 'Repent,' he intended that the entire life of believers should be one of repentance." Repentance is not a single event at conversion but an ongoing posture of the Christian life — a daily turning from self to God, from sin to grace.
