Psalm 46:10 — Be Still and Know

The psalm opens in chaos — mountains falling into the sea, nations raging, kingdoms tottering. And it is directly into that chaos that a voice commands: "Be still, and know that I am God." The Hebrew word for "be still" is raphah — not a mood, but an act: release your grip, cease striving, open your hands. God is not asking us to feel peaceful. He is commanding surrender — the deliberate release of control we were never meant to hold. The stillness is commanded precisely because the situation is not stable. This is not a peace that arrives after the storm; it is a peace that enters the storm on the basis of who God is.

We cannot manufacture stillness by trying harder to be calm. Stillness is a fruit of trusting someone bigger than our problem.

What does it mean that Jesus is the "Lamb of God"?

When John the Baptist cried "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!" he was making a claim loaded with centuries of meaning. The Passover lamb, the daily temple sacrifices, the Servant of Isaiah 53 led silently to slaughter — all were signposts pointing to this moment. "Takes away" is airōn (αἴρων), a present participle: the taking-away is continuous. And "sin" is singular — not a list of failures, but sin as the whole condition of the world's rebellion. He removes the root.

What does John 3:16 mean?

"For God so loved the world" — but οὕτως (houtōs) means "in this way," not merely "so much." The emphasis is on how God loved: by giving his μονογενής (monogenēs), his one and only Son. The gift is the costliest imaginable. Faith (πιστεύων, present participle — ongoing trust) is the one human act: receiving what God has given.
