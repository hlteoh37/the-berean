Walking through the Gospel of John

Currently in: The Gospel of John
March 19, 2026 2 min read

John 1:19-28 — The Voice

The religious leaders in Jerusalem sent priests to question John the Baptist: Who are you? His answer was striking — he refused every title they offered. Not the Christ. Not Elijah. Not the Prophet. All he would claim was this: "I am a voice crying out in the wilderness" (quoting Isaiah 40:3). He wasn't the message — just the messenger. Then he went even further: the one coming after him was so great that John wasn't even worthy to untie his sandals. In that culture, untying sandals was a slave's job — the lowest task imaginable. John wouldn't even claim that role for himself.

The greatest prophetic voice of his era spent it pointing entirely away from himself. True witness erases the witness.

From the Greek: John calls himself a phōnē boōntos (φωνὴ βοῶντος) — literally "a voice of one crying out." And the word for "worthy" is axios (ἄξιος) — meaning "of equal weight or value." John saw himself as weightless next to Jesus.

John 1:14-18 — God Moved In

"The Word became flesh and dwelt among us." That word "dwelt" is far richer than it sounds. It means something like "pitched his tent" or "set up camp" — and it deliberately echoes the Old Testament tabernacle, the tent where God's presence lived among Israel in the wilderness (Exodus 40). God didn't visit from a distance. He moved in. And what did people see when they looked at Jesus? "Grace and truth" — the very words God used to describe his own character to Moses on Mount Sinai (Exodus 34:6). The character of God, once heard as a voice on a mountain, now walked the streets in a human body. No one has ever seen God directly, John writes — but Jesus, who lives in the closest possible intimacy with the Father, has made him fully known.

Christ is God's own self-interpretation. If you want to know what God is like, look at Jesus.

From the Greek: The word for "dwelt" is eskēnōsen (ἐσκήνωσεν), from the same root as "tabernacle." And the word for "made known" in verse 18 is exēgēsato (ἐξηγήσατο) — the verb from which we get "exegesis." Jesus is the explanation of God.

John 1:6-13 — The Light the World Refused

God sent a man named John — not to be the Light, but to tell people about it. The real Light came into the world he had made, and the world didn't recognise him. Even his own people — Israel, who had the Scriptures, the promises, and centuries of prophets pointing to him — turned him away. It's a devastating summary: the Creator was rejected by his creation. But then comes the turn. To everyone who did receive him, who trusted in him, God gave the right to become his children. Not because of family lineage, human effort, or willpower — but because God himself chose to give them new life.

Rejection is the world's verdict on its Maker. But receiving him by faith crosses you from darkness into the family of God.

From the Greek: "His own" is hoi idioi (οἱ ἴδιοι) — meaning "his own people," those who belonged to him by covenant. And God gives believers exousia (ἐξουσία) — not just ability, but the legal right and standing to be called God's children.

John 1:1-5 — Before Everything, the Word

John's Gospel doesn't start with a birth story. It starts before everything — "In the beginning," echoing the very first words of the Bible (Genesis 1:1). Before the universe existed, the Word already was. He wasn't created alongside everything else; he was already there. John makes three staggering claims: the Word was with God (a real relationship, two persons face to face), the Word was God (fully divine, not a lesser being), and everything that exists was made through him. Then John says something beautiful: in this Word was life — not just biological existence, but life in the fullest, richest sense — and that life shone like light into the darkness. The darkness tried to extinguish it and failed. It always fails.

All of creation, all of life, all of light flows from the eternal Word. The darkness has never overcome it — and never will.

From the Greek: "Word" is logos (λόγος) — meaning not just a spoken word, but God's self-expression, his reason, his communication to the world. "Life" is zōē (ζωή) — a rich word meaning divine, eternal life (distinct from bios, everyday biological life). And the word for "overcome" (katelaben, κατέλαβεν) beautifully carries two meanings: the darkness neither understood nor conquered the light.

Now these Jews were more noble than those in Thessalonica; they received the word with all eagerness, examining the Scriptures daily to see if these things were so. — Acts 17:11 (ESV)
